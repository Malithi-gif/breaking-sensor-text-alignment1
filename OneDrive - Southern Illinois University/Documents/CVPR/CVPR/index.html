Breaking Sensor-Text Alignment

A visual demo for a cross-modal embedding poisoning attack on contrastive multimodal human activity recognition. The demo shows how small text-side embedding perturbations reduce cosine similarity, increase contrastive loss, and create semantic drift between sensor signals and activity descriptions.

What the demo explains

The target model aligns sensor windows and natural-language activity descriptions in a shared embedding space. The attack perturbs the text embedding during inference, making the correct sensor-text pair less similar and pushing the system toward incorrect activity interpretation.

📡

Attack effects shown in this website

These are the main visual effects you can present in your CVPR demo or poster QR-code website.

Effect 01

Similarity Drop

The correct sensor-text cosine similarity decreases after perturbation, meaning the matched activity description becomes less reliable.

Effect 02

Contrastive Loss Increase

The contrastive objective becomes larger because the correct pair is pushed apart in the shared embedding space.

Effect 03

Semantic Drift

The text embedding moves toward an incorrect target description, causing wrong or inconsistent activity interpretation.

Datasets and observed sensitivity

The demo summarizes the four datasets used in the paper and highlights how the attack behaves across motion and physiological sensing settings.

Motion HAR

WISDM

Shows activity-level variation. Downstairs is more vulnerable, while Standing and Walking require larger perturbations.

Motion HAR

MotionSense

Highly sensitive to small perturbations. Many activities become vulnerable at low epsilon values.

Device Heterogeneity

HHAR

Small positive perturbations can reliably disrupt sensor-text alignment across activities.

Physiological Signals

WESAD

Stress and non-stress text embeddings are highly sensitive to embedding-level perturbation.

Key takeaway

Contrastive sensor-text systems are powerful because they connect wearable signals with human-readable descriptions. However, this same alignment mechanism creates a new attack surface. A small embedding-level change can weaken the correct match, increase loss, and cause semantic misinterpretation.
